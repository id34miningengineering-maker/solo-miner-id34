Why this even exists

A solo miner actually hit block 938092.

February 24, 2026 · Block 938092 · Post-halving solo win

On February 24, 2026, a solo miner validated Bitcoin block 938092 and received a full 3.125 BTC subsidy plus transaction fees, for a total reward of about 3.12–3.13 BTC worth roughly 200,000 USD at the time. [web:2][web:3][web:4][web:5][web:6][web:7][web:9][web:10]

Multiple independent outlets and blockchain analytics providers confirmed that this block was mined by an individual, not a large pool, using only a small rented hashrate budget of around 75 USD. [web:2][web:3][web:4][web:9]

Statistically, solo-mined blocks make up well under one percent of Bitcoin’s annual block production, which means events like block 938092 are rare but real proofs that individual operators can still win the proof-of-work lottery. [web:7]

The ESP32 miners on this page do not promise profitability and cannot compete with industrial ASIC farms on expected value, but they connect directly to the Bitcoin network, point work at your own wallet, and let you observe the same process that produced block 938092—on your desk, in real time. [file:1][web:2][web:3][web:4][web:7]

In other words, the overwhelming majority of blocks will be found by professional operations, but non-zero probability still belongs to anyone who can submit valid hashes, even at tiny hashrates. These devices exist to make that asymmetry visible instead of abstract. [web:2][web:3][web:4][web:7]

The result is not a cloud contract or a custodial account. It is a low-power, transparent, open device that lets you or your kids watch shares, difficulty, and luck play out over months and years—with the remote but real possibility that the next improbable solo win looks a lot like block 938092.
